Modeling Geometrically Large Devices For geometrically large devices, the few lumped Spice components of a PI or TEE schematic may fail to represent distributed elements in the real, physical world.

For example, when the design application of the model is single-ended, 50% of the simple PI schematic's parasitics to ground are shorted when simulated, but in reality, in the measured device, only the very outer distributed parasitics to ground are shorted. Modeling Frequency-Dependent Losses
For frequencies above typically 35 GHz, frequency-dependent losses may show up and need to be modeled additionally to the previously presented methods with lumped Spice components. A smart method is to apply a special bridged TEE structure which maintains the characteristic impedance Z0, but adds frequency-dependent loss:

To better illustrate the behavior of this loss-adding circuit, let's simulate a lossless Spice delay line, featuring only the delay time TD and the characteristic impedance Z0, and add the Bridged TEE Schematic:


  • As expected, tuning the delay time TD affects only the phase of S21, with constant MAG(Sxy) = 1.
  • Tuning Lskin (1st order effect) and Rskin (2nd order effect) provides the frequency-dependent loss:
    the decrease of MAG(S21) with increasing frequency.
